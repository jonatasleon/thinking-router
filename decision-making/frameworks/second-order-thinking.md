# Framework Name

Second-order Thinking

## Core Mental Model

Immediate effects can be misleading. Better decisions come from tracing consequences beyond the first visible outcome.

## When To Use

- An option looks attractive in the short term.
- The user asks about long-term consequences.
- The decision may create delayed or compounding effects.

## When NOT To Use

- The decision is trivial and reversible.
- There is not enough clarity to define the initial action.
- The primary need is option scoring rather than consequence tracing.

## Key Diagnostic Questions

- What happens immediately if we do this?
- And then what?
- What changes at 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years?
- Which consequences are delayed, compounding, or hard to reverse?

## Step-by-Step Reasoning Process

1. State the candidate action clearly.
2. List first-order effects.
3. For each effect, ask "and then what?" and trace at least one deeper layer.
4. Separate short-, medium-, and long-term consequences.
5. Mark reversible versus irreversible consequences.
6. Mark locally positive but systemically negative effects.
7. Decide whether the option still looks good after higher-order review.
8. Return the consequence chain and decision implication.

## Example Application

If cutting QA speeds release, trace the second-order effects: more bugs, more support load, lower trust, and slower future shipping.
