# Framework Name

Impact-Effort Matrix

## Core Mental Model

Prioritization improves when you compare likely value against likely cost and avoid spending scarce effort on low-return work.

## When To Use

- The user needs to prioritize initiatives or ideas.
- There are many possible actions.
- Rough estimates are sufficient for sequencing.

## When NOT To Use

- Impact cannot be estimated at all.
- The issue is urgency rather than impact.
- The decision needs precise multi-factor comparison.

## Key Diagnostic Questions

- What is the likely impact of each option?
- What effort is required?
- Which items are quick wins?
- Which high-effort items deserve delay or decomposition?

## Step-by-Step Reasoning Process

1. List the candidate initiatives.
2. Define what "impact" and "effort" mean in this context.
3. Estimate each initiative on both dimensions.
4. Place each initiative into one of four zones: quick wins, major bets, fill-ins, or low-value drains.
5. Challenge any estimate that is based only on optimism.
6. Recommend what to do now, later, and not at all.

## Example Application

For a product backlog, identify a high-impact low-effort onboarding fix as a quick win while deferring a large redesign that is expensive and uncertain.
