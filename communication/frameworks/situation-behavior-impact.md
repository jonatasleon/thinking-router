# Framework Name

Situation-Behavior-Impact

## Core Mental Model

Feedback is clearer and less judgmental when it describes the specific situation, the observable behavior, and the impact rather than interpreting motives.

## When To Use

- The user needs to give feedback to a person.
- The conversation risks sounding accusatory.
- Specific observed behavior exists.

## When NOT To Use

- The user lacks a concrete observed situation.
- The goal is an executive summary or memo.
- The message is mainly about strategy rather than behavior.

## Key Diagnostic Questions

- What exact situation are we referring to?
- What observable behavior occurred?
- What impact did it have on me, others, or the work?
- Is intent known or only assumed?

## Step-by-Step Reasoning Process

1. Name the situation precisely.
2. Describe only the observable behavior; avoid motive language.
3. State the impact in practical or emotional terms.
4. If useful, ask about intent rather than assuming it.
5. Invite reflection or discussion about future change.
6. Return a message that is specific, respectful, and non-judgmental.

## Example Application

"In yesterday's review meeting, when you interrupted the customer twice, it made it harder for us to hear their full concern and made the conversation feel tense. Can you share what was going on for you in that moment?"
