# Framework Name

Six Thinking Hats

## Core Mental Model

Better decisions emerge when you deliberately separate different modes of thinking instead of blending emotion, judgment, data, and creativity all at once.

## When To Use

- A group or model is stuck in one perspective.
- The task needs balanced exploration before choosing.
- Conflict comes from mixing critique, ideas, and feelings together.

## When NOT To Use

- The task is a simple analytical comparison.
- There are only two clear options and defined criteria.
- Speed matters more than perspective coverage.

## Key Diagnostic Questions

- What facts do we know?
- What emotions or intuitions are present?
- What risks or weaknesses matter?
- What upside or benefits are possible?
- What novel options exist?
- What process should guide the discussion?

## Step-by-Step Reasoning Process

1. State the decision or question.
2. Run the hats one at a time.
3. White hat: list facts, data gaps, and uncertainties.
4. Red hat: note feelings, intuitions, or stakeholder reactions.
5. Black hat: identify risks, constraints, and failure modes.
6. Yellow hat: identify value, upside, and enabling conditions.
7. Green hat: generate alternatives or improvements.
8. Blue hat: synthesize what matters and define the next decision step.
9. Return a recommendation only after all relevant hats have been covered.

## Example Application

When evaluating a new market entry, separate known market facts, team fears, downside risks, upside potential, new entry approaches, and then synthesize them into a recommendation.
